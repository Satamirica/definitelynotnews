How to use the project in education

Definitely Not News is designed to be used not just for independent viewing, but as a tool for media literacy, journalism, literature or social studies lessons – with no special preparation required.

The fairy tale format reduces resistance from the audience: students and schoolchildren don't feel they are being taught to "distrust the media" – they simply watch two short episodes about the same event and start noticing the difference themselves. That is the key: understanding comes through experience, not explanation.

Each episode pair runs two to three minutes in total. After watching, just five discussion questions are enough to launch a substantial group conversation. The theoretical breakdown on the website is brought in afterwards – as a way of naming what the group just felt intuitively.

The project is available in Russian, English and Kazakh, making it applicable in a range of educational contexts.

Applications

Where to use the project

📱
Media literacy
The project directly illustrates key media literacy concepts: agenda setting, framing, source selection, visual fakes and echo chambers. Each theme has a theory page with sources.
📚
Literature and narrative
Each episode works with the narrative structure of a fairy tale. The project shows how the same text generates different interpretations depending on the narrator's position.
🗞️
Journalism and communications
For journalism students – a practicum on editorial decisions. Every episode demonstrates choices around source selection, tone, structure and how these shape the picture of an event.
🧠
Social studies and critical thinking
The fake news themes (Block 2) work well in digital literacy and social studies contexts: the anchoring effect, echo chambers, the speed of disinformation spread – all shown through concrete examples.
🌐
Language lessons
The Russian, English and Kazakh versions allow episodes to be used in language classes as material for analysing style, register and professional media language.
👨‍👩‍👧
Family viewing
The short format and fairy tale basis make the project suitable for watching with children over 12. A good starting point for a conversation about how news works.
Methodology

How to run a lesson on one theme

One episode pair – about 45 minutes including viewing and discussion.

01
Watch the first episode – without discussion
Ask the group to simply watch the first outlet's episode, without knowing there will be a second. The initial reaction before comparison is the teaching material.
02
Watch the second episode
Now the group sees the same event through a different outlet. Reactions will start during the viewing – that's fine. The educator's role at this stage is to observe, not to explain.
03
Five questions for discussion
Discussion questions
1What did each outlet choose as the main point of this story?
2Whose voices were heard in each episode – and whose were not?
3What emotion did each piece trigger? The same or different?
4What stayed off camera in each episode?
5Did your view of the event change after the second episode?
04
Bring in the theory
After the discussion, open the theory page for this theme on the website and show how what the group noticed intuitively is described in academic research. Theory comes from practice – not the other way round.
05
Transfer task
Ask students to find a real media item – a news article, post or report – and apply the same five questions. This embeds the skill through independent work with real content.
