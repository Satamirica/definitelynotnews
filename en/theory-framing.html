What a frame is

In 1993 American researcher Robert Entman published a paper that became one of the most cited in media studies. He proposed the concept of framing – the way journalists and editorial teams select, emphasise and interpret information when constructing a media text.

A frame is a lens placed over an event. It determines exactly what we see when we look at a situation: its causes, its participants, its meaning and possible solutions. The same fact can be placed in different frames – and it becomes a fundamentally different story without ceasing to be true.

«Framing essentially involves selection and salience. To frame is to select some aspects of a perceived reality and make them more salient in a communicating text.»– Robert Entman, Framing: Toward Clarification of a Fractured Paradigm, 1993

Entman's four functions of a frame

Four functions of a frame
1
Defining the problem. What exactly is the "problem" here? The frame decides whether the ball is a successful event, a detective mystery or a symptom of social inequality.
2
Causal interpretation. Who or what caused the situation? The frame determines whether Cinderella is a lucky winner, a victim or someone with a dark past.
3
Moral evaluation. How should we feel about this? Pride in the monarchy, anxiety about security, or a question for the neighbours who stayed silent for years.
4
Recommendation. What should be done? Congratulate the court, investigate the circumstances of the stranger's appearance, or start a conversation about why no adult was there.

Cinderella: one ball, four frames

In the project, the same fairy tale ball becomes four different stories depending on which frame each outlet uses. None of the frames is a lie – each is based on real facts. But the result is fundamentally different in each case.

Why the frame is invisible

The key feature of framing is that it works invisibly. The reader doesn't perceive the frame as an interpretation – they perceive it as reality. When The Royal Herald writes "the prince witnessed the match" and The First Crier writes "servants cannot explain how she got in", both are talking about the same slipper and the same evening. But the reader of the first piece is thinking about the successful conclusion of a search, while the reader of the second is thinking about a security breach.
