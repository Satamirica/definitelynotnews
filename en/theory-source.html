Source and Authority

Why the choice of source is already a position

A journalist who quotes an official source does not necessarily share that source's viewpoint. But the very act of citing one source at the expense of another is already an editorial decision with consequences. Paul Lazarsfeld in The People's Choice (1944) described the two-step flow of communication: information flows from media to "opinion leaders" and through them to the broader audience. Who media choose as opinion leaders – who they give the floor to – is their source policy.

Stuart Hall in Encoding/Decoding (1973) went further: the encoding of a message itself already contains a position. There is no neutral source – everyone speaks from a particular social position with particular interests and particular knowledge. When media choose an "official" source, they are not simply transmitting facts – they are legitimising a particular version of reality.

«Encoding always entails choices. And these choices are never neutral – they reproduce certain dominant meanings even when they appear to simply transmit facts.»– Stuart Hall, Encoding/Decoding, 1973

The hierarchy of sources

In media practice there is an informal but stable source hierarchy. At the top are official institutions – government, courts, major organisations. Below them are recognised experts with academic or professional credentials. Further down are ordinary participants in events, eyewitnesses, "ordinary people". On the periphery are those considered unreliable or marginal sources.

This hierarchy is reproduced in media texts so consistently that readers perceive it as natural. Yet it is a social construct: the fact that an "official expert" is cited more often does not mean their knowledge is more accurate or closer to the event.

Source hierarchy in media text
1
Official institutions. Royal chancellery, forest guard, building authority. Their words are cited as fact, not as version.
2
Recognised experts. Court sage, poultry breeders' society, master craftsmen. Their opinion is considered authoritative by default.
3
Witnesses and participants. Neighbours, stable hands, passers-by at the gate. Their words are cited with hedges: "according to", "as they claim".
4
Marginalised sources. Baba Yaga, anonymous readers, "those who were not at the hearing". Their knowledge is ignored or devalued through their status.

Ivan Tsarevich: whoever speaks is right

In the project, Ivan Tsarevich and the Firebird shows how the same outcome (Ivan completed the task) looks fundamentally different depending on which sources each outlet cites. The Royal Herald builds its piece around the royal chancellery and the court sage. The First Crier goes to stable hands, passers-by at the gate, travellers from the border forest – and to Baba Yaga, who met Ivan personally.

Authority as a substitute for proof

One of the key mechanisms connected to source choice is using authority as a substitute for verification. If the source is authoritative, their claim is accepted as true without additional checking. The court sage Prokhor cites chronicles from three centuries ago – and this is accepted as sufficient grounds. Baba Yaga met Ivan personally – but her words do not appear in the official protocol.
