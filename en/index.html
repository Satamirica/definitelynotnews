About

Fairy tales don't lie. They just leave things out.

Definitely Not News is an educational media project about how manipulation works in media and how fake news spreads. Instead of real news we use fairy tales: everyone knows them, they don't trigger resistance, and they are built exactly like any media text.

The same fairy tale event is covered by two fictional outlets with different editorial positions. Neither of them lies outright. But each chooses its own angle, its own sources, its own way of speaking – and this changes everything.

How to watch

Three steps to media literacy

01
Watch both episodes
Each episode pair covers the same fairy tale event. The Herald and the Crier release their takes on the same theme – watch both in sequence.
02
Look for the difference
What details does each outlet choose? Who do they quote? What stays off camera? Don't ask "who is right" – ask "why do they tell it this way".
03
Apply to real life
The skill practised on fairy tales works with any media text. The same techniques – agenda setting, framing, source selection – appear in every news story you read.

Why fairy tales

The filter that doesn't work on fairy tales

When you read a news story about a real event, you already know who you trust and who you double-check. That filter activates automatically — and prevents you from noticing what happens at the structural level: who got a voice, what became the headline, where the story begins and where the editorial team decided to end it.

With a fairy tale, that filter doesn't work. You don't know in advance whose side to take, so you start noticing techniques rather than content. And those techniques, it turns out, are the same in the fairy-tale kingdom and in any real newsroom.

Some episodes seem absurd at first. Then something similar turns up in a real news feed — and the absurdity suddenly stops feeling like a fairy-tale phenomenon.
