Narrative as the foundation of media text

Vladimir Propp in Morphology of the Folktale (1928) showed that all fairy tales follow the same structure: there is a hero, a villain, a donor, a trial and a reward. Forty years later, French semiotician Roland Barthes transferred this analysis to media texts – and found that news is built exactly the same way.

Any media text about an event answers questions: who is the hero here, who is the villain and who is the victim? These roles do not follow automatically from the facts – the editorial team assigns them. And this assignment shapes everything that follows.

«Narrative structures in media texts reproduce basic mythological schemas through which society makes sense of its conflicts. Hero and villain are not characters – they are functions.»– Roland Barthes, Introduction to the Structural Analysis of Narratives, 1966

How media assign roles

Role assignment happens through specific editorial tools. Word choice already sets the role: "attacked" versus "came looking for food". Order of presentation sets the role: if the story begins with destroyed houses, the wolf is already a villain before the reader learns anything about him. Source choice sets the role: if we only hear from the pigs, we only see their version of the conflict.

Tools for role assignment in media text
1
Word choice. "Attack" or "conflict", "victim" or "participant", "threat" or "competitor" – each choice already contains an evaluation.
2
Order of presentation. Whether the story begins with destruction or with backstory determines the reader's viewpoint on everything that follows.
3
Context or its absence. A wolf in the forest without context is a threat. A wolf on its historic territory from which it was displaced is an entirely different story.
4
Moral or its absence. Does the piece close with a lesson – and what lesson? "Build well" or "make sure everyone has equal access to materials" – two different conclusions from the same facts.

Three Little Pigs: villain or the displaced?

In the project the Three Little Pigs show how the same wolf receives fundamentally different roles depending on the editorial angle. The Royal Herald constructs a narrative of "hard work versus carelessness": Practical Pig is the hero who foresaw the threat, the wolf is chaos personified. The First Crier shifts the perspective: this is a territorial conflict in which the wolf is the displaced party and the pigs are those who occupied its habitat.

Neither version is a lie. Both rely on real facts. But the result is fundamentally different: the reader of the first piece sees a victory over a threat, the reader of the second sees competition over resources.
