Emotional Impact

📖 Father Frost · 4 episodes

When a feeling arrives before a fact, it becomes a filter. The emotion a piece triggers determines how the reader interprets everything that follows.

Why emotion matters more than fact

Research in cognitive psychology and media communication has long established that emotionally charged messages are processed faster, remembered better and persuade more effectively than neutral ones. Leszek Voitasik in Psychology of Political Propaganda (1981) described this mechanism from the perspective of persuasive communication: emotion creates a predisposition that shapes the interpretation of all subsequent information.

Pierre Bourdieu in On Television (1996) showed the systemic aspect of this phenomenon: television systematically favours dramatic and emotionally rich material – not because journalists are deliberately manipulating, but because such material receives more attention. This is a structural incentive that shapes the entire media landscape.

«Television addresses emotions, not reason. This is not malice – it is a structural feature of the medium that has political consequences regardless of intentions.»– Pierre Bourdieu, On Television, 1996

How the same event triggers different emotions

The key observation is that a media text's emotional impact is determined not by the event itself but by how it is presented. The same facts can produce warmth, anxiety, pride or outrage – depending on which details the editorial team selects, what tone the presenter adopts and where in the story the account begins.

The emotional impact mechanism
1
The editorial team chooses an "entry emotion". The team decides whether the story begins with anxiety, admiration or outrage. This first emotion becomes the backdrop for everything that follows.
2
Details intensify the emotion. "A girl walked through the forest" and "a girl walked through the forest in twenty degrees of frost without food or shelter" are the same fact with very different emotional weight.
3
Emotion becomes a filter. A reader already feeling anxiety interprets subsequent facts through that anxiety. A reader feeling warmth interprets them through warmth.
4
The conclusion follows from the emotion. "It ended well" or "next time we might not be so lucky" – both conclusions are logical, but only from different emotional states.

Father Frost: one story, two emotions

In the project, the four Father Frost episodes show how the same event – a girl in the winter forest – triggers fundamentally different emotional responses depending on the editorial angle. The Herald in the first two episodes works through warmth and uplift – these are comforting emotions that lead to a specific conclusion about behaviour. The Crier works through anxiety and an open question – the same facts, a different emotion, a different conclusion.
