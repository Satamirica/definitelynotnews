Agenda Setting

📖 Little Red Riding Hood · 4 episodes

Media don't tell us what to think – they tell us what to think about. The choice of which event becomes the main story is already an editorial decision with consequences.

What agenda setting means

In 1972 American researchers Maxwell McCombs and Donald Shaw published a study that changed the understanding of how mass media work. Observing US presidential elections, they found a striking pattern: the topics that media covered most heavily were perceived by voters as the most important – regardless of whether they were actually significant.

This gave rise to agenda-setting theory. Its core idea is simple and revolutionary: media don't tell us what to think – they tell us what to think about. An outlet doesn't impose an opinion directly. Instead it determines which events exist in our picture of the world and which remain invisible.

«The press may not be successful much of the time in telling people what to think, but it is stunningly successful in telling its readers what to think about.»– Bernard Cohen, The Press and Foreign Policy, 1963

How it works in practice

Imagine two events happen today: a criminal is caught and a systemic failure in the city's security apparatus is discovered. Both events are real. Both concern the same incident. But one outlet makes its headline "criminal caught" while another leads with "system failure". Neither is lying. But each constructs a fundamentally different picture of reality.

This is agenda setting: the editorial team decides what counts as an event, which aspect of the situation deserves attention and which stays off camera. These decisions are made every time a journalist sits down to write.

How the agenda-setting mechanism works
1
An event occurs. Reality always has several simultaneous aspects – threat, response, consequences, context, perpetrators, victims.
2
The editorial team chooses an angle. Which aspect becomes the headline? This decision is shaped by editorial policy, audience, sources and dozens of other factors.
3
The rest fades or disappears. What was not written about does not exist for most readers. Media reality is built from what made the agenda.
4
The audience takes this as the full picture. The reader doesn't know what they didn't see. That is why agenda setting works invisibly.

Little Red Riding Hood: one event, two agendas

In the project, the agenda-setting mechanism is shown through four episodes with Little Red Riding Hood. One event: a girl walks through the forest, meets a wolf, woodcutters arrive in time, everyone is alive. But The Royal Herald and The First Crier make different things the main story – and as a result the viewer who watches both sees two fundamentally different "events".

How to recognise agenda setting

The skill starts with a simple question: what is not written about here? When reading any piece, ask yourself: why did this particular aspect become the headline? Who benefits from attention being focused here? What questions does this piece not ask? What happened before the story that is being told here begins?

Key terms
Agenda SettingThe ability of media to determine which topics are considered important in society.
Media realityThe picture of the world constructed by media – not identical to reality but influencing how it is perceived.
SilencingThe deliberate or unintentional exclusion of a topic or aspect from an outlet's agenda.
Agenda closureThe editorial decision to declare a topic exhausted regardless of the actual development of the situation.
